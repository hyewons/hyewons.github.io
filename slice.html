Slice

A mobile application that reinvents the billing process at a restaurant by giving users the control to split and pay bill without the waiter's interaction.

Type: Personal side project
Duration: 8 weeks
Role: User Research, Competitive Analysis, Low-Fi & High-Fi Prototyping, Interaction Design, Usability Testing
Tools: Sketch, Photoshop, Principle, Origami Studio

The dining experience should be less painful!

The idea of eating as a social activity is nothing new. However, there seems to be significant inefficiencies that prevent the diners from having pleasant dining experience. From waiting to get seated to fighting for waiter’s attention to the unfortunate task of splitting the bill, that dinner night can turn out to be a nightmare. Meanwhile, technology in the restaurant industry is rapidly becoming the new normal as 81% of the restaurants use a point-of-sales (POS) system. Today’s POS systems like Breadcrumb and Square are advanced enough to feature multiple card processing, labor management, and integration with other software. We should leverage current POS system technology to enhance the dining experience.

User Journey

We mapped out an end-to end user journey to visualize areas where frustrations might arise. The sources of frustrations are marked in red.

User Research

To narrow down the project scope, we used interviews, contextual inquiry and survey data. We conducted 5 semi-structured interviews with frequent diners, including former waiters, to understand their habits & experience from both sides. For contextual inquiry, we were able to observe a big group meal from an end-to-end process. After uncovering common frustrations about ordering and billing process, we sent out online survey with a focus on those. The insights from the research were critical in narrowing down on designing a solution for a better billing experience.

Key Takeaways

Project Scope

Based on our findings, waiter is appreciated during ordering as it provides positive human interaction but not when it comes to payment. Therefore, we need to focus on giving users more control over the billing process.

Competitive Analysis

After narrowing down our scope to designing a solution for billing process, we observed other mobile applications that help users to pay and split bill at a restaurant. After putting them into 2x2 matrix, we were able to find our market opportunity.

Solution

We iterated on the service concept, and made sure it aligns with our users' needs and values from the research. Our final solution is a mobile app that improves efficiency of the billing process by enabling the users to take care of the bill by themselves and thus helping the waiters to focus more on other tasks.

Assumption #1

We are assuming diners eating at high-end, expensive restaurants will be receiving top-notch service by their waiter. Therefore, we are targeting users who dine at casual, average-priced sit-down restaurants.

Assumption #2

Restaurants will have advanced POS system so user's phone can connect to the restaurant. When connected, the POS data (order details & bill) will be sent directly to the user’s phone.

Paper Prototype

We chose to create paper prototypes to quickly test and verify our idea with the users.

Usability Testing

We tested our paper prototypes by conducting guerrilla usability testing and luckily, we received diverse feedbacks. The participants were asked to complete “Pay by item” and "Split evenly" feature in a hypothetical scenario. They provided feedbacks on the user flow, content and discoverability of UI choices.

Based on our test findings, we refined the user flow and design elements and reexamined user's language in a restaurant context. Then, we created additional scenarios for certain edge cases such as unpaid items and sharing a multiple-quantity item.

Scenario Walkthrough

Jane is dining out with her best friends, Ian and Paul at Little Thai today. They get seated and the waiter comes to the table to take the order. Along with the order, Jane, who is the "bill starter" today, launches Slice and gives her id number to the waiter.

The waiter inputs Jane's id number to the POS system and successfully checks her in. He then adds order items to the bill.

The best friends are done eating. Instead of calling the waiter for the bill, Jane launches Slice and adds "Best Friends" group to start bill splitting.

Jane's friend Ian receives a notification that he has been added to the bill at Little Thai. Ian agrees to join the bill.

The best friends view the bill and decide to split the bill by item. Jane shares Thai Noodles with Ian and Paul, selects two portion of Mixed Vegetables and one Crab Delight. After she confirms the subtotal, Jane tips the waiter and finishes her total payment of $39.65.

Everyone is done with their payment, but some item is left unpaid. Ian forgot to pay for his Grilled Salmon! Ian selects the item directly on the screen and makes his payment to avoid charge on Jane's card.

Split Evenly Scenario

Jane had family-style dinner with her coworkers. They want to split the bill evenly by six people.

Design Explorations

We realized that the most important function of this real-time interactive app lies on solving tricky scenarios that can occur in bill splitting situations.

Challenge: Jane and Paul shared one Mixed Vegetable

The first version (left) solves selecting from multiple quantity item. But what if Jane had one Mixed Vegetable and shared the second one with Paul? This scenario prompted the second version (middle) where the drop down arrow seperates Mixed Vegetables into three items. This looked like the most ideal version, but after some evaluation, the drop down box is not intuitive and takes up too much space. We wanted an interaction that keeps the simple interaction of the first version and accomodates these edge case scenarios. We finalized with the third screen (right), where users are given the simple interaction as a default and additional options to split item manually.

Challenge: Grilled Salmon is left unpaid

Users paying all items on the bill perfectly is an ideal scenario. However, there are always human errors. What if some item is not paid and the bill starter gets charged for someone else's food? Then no one will want to be the bill starter. This was a critical problem to solve. The first version (left) inhibits the users from payment until all items have been claimed. However, this creates another "waiting" moment that we wanted to remove in the first place. We finalized with the second version (right) where everyone is shown the unpaid item(s) immediately after their payment so they can settle the issue at the spot.

Reflection &Future Iteration

Sharing a meal is about getting together, without tech in between. We had to streamline the app to minimize screen time during the meal and it required a lot of logistic considerations on both stakeholders. Also we made sure the solution lets diners to focus on the meal and comes into play only when diners want to pay.

As my first time working with someone remotely, my teammates and I developed few methods that allow us to collaborate in an efficient fashion. We iterated on each other’s ideas to combine all perspectives, and always seek answers from our users when we cannot find one on our own.

If I had more time, I would run additional usability testing to make sure our solution solves problems in complicated situations. I'm sure there are other situations that we missed because bill splitting is complicated!
